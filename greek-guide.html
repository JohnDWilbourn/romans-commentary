Reference Guide

A Guide to the Greek New Testament

For the serious reader who wants to understand what the text actually says

The New Testament was written in Greek. Not the classical Greek of Plato and Thucydides, and not the Greek you might encounter in a modern Athens café, but a form of the language called Koine — the common tongue of the eastern Mediterranean world in the first century, the lingua franca of merchants, soldiers, administrators, and scholars across the Roman empire. It was a living, flexible, remarkably precise instrument, and God chose it deliberately for the permanent written deposit of His revelation to the Church Age.

This guide exists for one reason: so that the serious reader of this commentary can engage with the Greek text on its own terms. You do not need to become a Greek scholar. You do not need to read the New Testament in Greek to profit from it. But you should understand why the Greek matters, what it can tell you that no English translation can fully convey, how the language is structured, where the text we are working from comes from, and what the key terms mean in the original — particularly the terms that carry the full weight of a doctrine.

The Greek text used throughout this commentary is the NA28 / SBLGNT critical text tradition. Why that matters, and what the alternatives are, is the first question this guide addresses.

"This is eternal life: that they may know You, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom You have sent." John 17:3 — ESV

To know God — not merely to know about Him — is the driving purpose of this commentary and the reason the Greek text is worth your careful attention.

Section I

Why the Greek Text Matters

Every translation is an interpretation

When a translator renders a Greek sentence into English, he is making decisions. He is choosing which of several possible meanings to carry across, how to handle a Greek tense that has no English equivalent, whether to translate a word literally or dynamically, and how much of the original precision to preserve or sacrifice for the sake of readable prose. These are legitimate decisions, and skilled translators make them well. But the decisions are never neutral. Every translation reflects a theological tradition, a philosophy of translation, and the limits of the target language.

This does not mean translations are untrustworthy. The ESV, used as the primary English text in this commentary, is an excellent and carefully produced translation. But it means that when the commentary says a Greek word means something the English does not fully capture — when it distinguishes between γνῶσις and ἐπίγνωσις, or unpacks the force of a Greek perfect tense — you are seeing the text at a level no translation can fully render. The Greek is not a curiosity or an ornament. It is the thing itself.

The question of which Greek text

Not all Greek New Testaments are the same. This fact surprises many readers, but it is simply the reality of how ancient documents survive. No original manuscripts of the New Testament exist. What we have are thousands of handwritten copies — manuscripts — made over roughly fifteen centuries, from approximately the second century through the fifteenth. They are in extraordinary agreement on the overwhelming substance of the text. But they disagree in thousands of small details, and in a handful of passages, in ways that matter for interpretation.

Two major traditions have shaped how Christians read the Greek New Testament.

The Textus Receptus (TR)
The "received text" — compiled by Erasmus in 1516 from a small number of late medieval manuscripts, subsequently revised and printed in various editions. This is the Greek text behind the King James Version (1611) and its descendants. It represents a late Byzantine manuscript tradition.
The Critical Text (CT)
Compiled from the full range of available manuscript evidence — papyri, uncials, minuscules, and early translations — using established text-critical methodology to reconstruct the most likely original reading. The NA28 (Nestle-Aland, 28th edition) and SBLGNT represent this tradition. It is used in the ESV, NASB, NIV, and most modern translations.

Why this commentary uses the critical text

The manuscript evidence is not ambiguous on this point. The oldest and most geographically diverse witnesses to the New Testament text — including papyri dating to the second and third centuries, preserved in the dry climate of Egypt — consistently support readings found in the critical text. The Byzantine tradition from which the TR derives is well-attested, internally consistent, and venerable; but it is late, and where it diverges from the older witnesses, the weight of evidence favors the critical text.

The KJV is a magnificent translation of a text that was the best available in 1611. But Erasmus did not have access to Codex Sinaiticus, Codex Vaticanus, Papyrus 46, or the hundreds of other early manuscripts identified and analyzed in the four centuries since. The critical text does. This commentary works from the best available evidence, which is the only intellectually honest position available to a serious student of the Word of God.

Note on Textual Variants

What is actually at stake

The differences between the TR and the critical text are real, but their significance is often overstated in popular debate. No major Christian doctrine depends on a textually disputed passage. The variants affect wording, phrasing, and occasionally the inclusion or exclusion of a verse or short passage — but the doctrinal substance of the New Testament is stable across all manuscript traditions. The reason for preferring the critical text is not theological defensiveness but scholarly integrity: we want to know, as precisely as possible, what was originally written.

Section II

The Greek alphabet has twenty-four letters. It is the ancestor of the Latin alphabet (and therefore of English), so several letters will look immediately familiar. Others are entirely new. Learning to recognize them is not difficult — it is a matter of an afternoon's attention — and it pays dividends immediately: you will be able to read Greek terms in the commentary and recognize them when they recur, rather than treating them as opaque foreign objects.

Breathing marks and accents

Every Greek word beginning with a vowel carries a breathing mark. The smooth breathing (᾿) is silent. The rough breathing (῾) adds an h sound before the vowel: ἁμαρτία (hamartia — sin) begins with a rough breathing, giving it the initial h sound. Words beginning with rho (ρ) always carry a rough breathing, which is why Greek ῥήτωρ (rhetor — orator) gives us the English word "rhetoric."

Greek words also carry accent marks — acute (´), grave (`), and circumflex (῀) — which in classical Greek indicated pitch, and in Koine and later tradition indicate stress. For the purposes of reading this commentary, accents are visible on Greek terms primarily as aids to pronunciation and to distinguish words that are otherwise identically spelled.

A note on diphthongs

Greek frequently combines vowels into diphthongs — two vowels pronounced as a single sound. The most common are: αι (as in "aisle"), αυ (as in "how"), ει (as in "they"), οι (as in "oil"), ου (as in "food"), and υι (rare). Recognizing these will help considerably when reading Greek terms aloud.

Section III

How Greek Grammar Works

Greek is an inflected language. Where English relies primarily on word order to convey meaning ("the dog bit the man" means something very different from "the man bit the dog"), Greek encodes meaning in the endings of words. This gives Greek a flexibility and precision that English cannot match — a sentence can be arranged in almost any order without changing its meaning, because the endings tell you which noun is the subject and which is the object. It also means that the endings themselves carry enormous interpretive weight.

What follows is not a grammar lesson in the formal sense. It is an orientation — enough to understand what the commentary is talking about when it discusses a case, a tense, or a mood.

Nouns: the case system

Greek nouns change their endings to show their grammatical function in a sentence. Each distinct form is called a case. There are five cases in Koine Greek.

CaseFunctionEnglish equivalent / example
NominativeSubjectThe one performing the action. ὁ θεὸς ἠγάπησεν — God loved.
GenitiveOf / PossessionRelationship or source. ἀγάπη τοῦ θεοῦ — the love of God. Enormously flexible; often translated with "of" but covering a wide range of relationships.
DativeTo / For / By / InThe indirect object; also instrument or location. πιστεύω τῷ θεῷ — I believe in God. χάριτί ἐστε σεσῳσμένοι — you are saved by grace.
AccusativeDirect objectThe one receiving the action. ἠγάπησεν τὸν κόσμον — He loved the world.
VocativeDirect addressCalling out to someone. κύριε — Lord!

The genitive case deserves special mention because it is among the most theologically loaded in the New Testament. When Paul writes of the δικαιοσύνη θεοῦ — "the righteousness of God" (Romans 1:17) — the genitive can mean the righteousness that belongs to God, the righteousness that comes from God, the righteousness that God requires, or the righteousness that God is. These are not the same thing, and the exegetical and theological debate over which meaning Paul intends in Romans is centuries old. A single Greek case inflection opens up that entire discussion.

Verbs: tense, aspect, mood, and voice

Greek verbs are among the most information-dense elements of any language. A single Greek verb form encodes person (first, second, third), number (singular, plural), tense, aspect, mood, and voice — all simultaneously, in a single word. English routinely requires several words to convey what Greek conveys in one.

For the reader of this commentary, the most important features are verbal aspect (what the tense communicates about the kind of action) and mood (the speaker's relationship to the reality of the action).

Verbal aspect: the heart of Greek tense

Greek tense is fundamentally about aspect — the way the speaker portrays an action — not merely about time. There are three primary aspects.

TenseAspectWhat it communicates
PresentImperfectiveAction portrayed as ongoing, continuous, or repeated. περιπατεῖτε (Gal. 5:16) — "keep on walking by means of the Spirit" — a continuous mandate, not a one-time act.
AoristPerfective / PunctiliarAction viewed as a whole, as a single event, without reference to its duration. The most common narrative tense. "He believed" — one definite act. The aorist does not always mean instantaneous; it treats the action as a complete unit.
PerfectStative / ResultativeA past action with a present, continuing result. This is the most theologically loaded tense in the NT. σεσῳσμένοι (Eph. 2:8) — "you have been saved [and remain saved]." τετέλεσται (John 19:30) — "it is finished [and permanently so]." The perfect tense communicates irreversibility.
ImperfectPast imperfectiveContinuous or repeated action in past time. "He was teaching" — an ongoing past activity.
FuturePredictiveAction anticipated as future. In Greek, the future is primarily temporal rather than aspectual.
PluperfectPast stativeLike the perfect, but the completed action and its result are both in past time. Relatively rare in the NT.
The perfect tense is among the most powerful instruments of theological precision in the Greek New Testament. When a writer uses it, he is asserting not merely that something happened, but that its result stands permanently in place.

Mood

Mood expresses the speaker's view of the action's relationship to reality.

MoodMeaningExample
IndicativeActual / declarativeA statement of fact (or assertion of fact). "God is faithful." The dominant mood in NT epistolary prose.
ImperativeCommandA direct command or prohibition. Present imperative = continue doing. Aorist imperative = do this (as a complete act). The distinction between "stop doing" and "do not begin" is often present imperative vs. aorist.
SubjunctivePotential / contingentAction as possible, probable, or contingent. Common in purpose clauses ("in order that…") and conditional sentences.
OptativeWish / remote possibilityExpresses a wish or a very remote possibility. μὴ γένοιτο — Paul's emphatic "May it never be!" / "God forbid!" (Romans 3:4, 6; 6:2, 15, et al.).

Voice

Greek has three voices: active (the subject performs the action), passive (the subject receives the action), and middle (the subject acts with some reference to itself, or participates in the results of the action). The middle voice has no exact English equivalent and is often collapsed into the active in translation, but it can communicate reflexive action, self-interest, or intensified personal involvement that a straight active form does not.

Participles and infinitives

Greek makes extensive use of participles — verbal adjectives that carry both the qualities of a verb (tense, voice, aspect) and an adjective (modifying a noun). They are ubiquitous in Paul. A participle can express time, cause, means, manner, condition, or concession — often in a single form. When this commentary discusses a participle, it is usually to clarify what relationship the participle bears to the main verb of the sentence, which the English translation may not make explicit.

Infinitives in Greek are verbal nouns. They can serve as the subject or object of a verb, express purpose, or complement a main verb. Like participles, they carry tense and voice, and their aspect communicates whether the action is viewed as ongoing or complete.

Prepositions and compound words

Greek prepositions are extraordinarily precise when used with specific cases. The same preposition can mean different things depending on whether its object is in the genitive, dative, or accusative. Beyond their use with nouns, prepositions frequently form the prefix of compound verbs, dramatically intensifying or redirecting the meaning of the root verb. The pair γνῶσις and ἐπίγνωσις is a case in point: the prefix ἐπί (upon, toward, at) transforms ordinary knowledge into full, directed, applied knowledge — a difference this commentary treats as doctrinally decisive.

Section IV

How to Read a Lexicon Entry

A lexicon is a Greek-English dictionary, but it is considerably more than a list of equivalents. A well-constructed lexicon entry documents the range of meaning a word carries across its usage in classical, Hellenistic, and biblical Greek, distinguishes different semantic senses with examples, and points you to specific occurrences so you can verify the usage yourself. Learning to read one is a skill that takes an hour to acquire and rewards a lifetime of use.

The standard lexicon for New Testament Greek is BDAG — A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament and Other Early Christian Literature, by Bauer, Danker, Arndt, and Gingrich (3rd edition, 2000). It is the professional tool used by every serious NT scholar. Below is an explanation of how a BDAG entry is structured, using a simplified model based on the word πίστις (faith).

πίστις, εως, ἡ
PISTIS, -EOS, HĒ  |  Noun, Feminine  |  Occurs 243× in NT
Root meaning: from πείθω (peithō) — to persuade, to trust, to be convinced of.
① Trust, confidence, reliance — directed toward a person or proposition as worthy of confidence. The active exercise of belief. "The faith of Abraham" (Rom. 4:16). Rom. 1:17; 3:22, 25, 26, 28; Gal. 2:16; Eph. 2:8
② The body of doctrine believed — the objective content of what Christians believe; the faith as a deposit. "Contend for the faith once delivered to the saints" (Jude 3). Jude 3; 1 Tim. 4:1; Gal. 1:23
③ Faithfulness, reliability — of God: His trustworthiness. "The faithfulness of God" (Rom. 3:3). Rom. 3:3; Gal. 5:22
Note: In theological use, sense ① is primary. The word is non-meritorious by nature — trust is not a human achievement but a response to evidence. Eph. 2:8: διὰ πίστεως (through faith) — the channel, not the ground, of salvation.

Several features of that entry are worth noting. First, the lexical form: πίστις, εως, ἡ. The first form is the nominative singular (the dictionary form). The second (εως) gives the genitive singular ending, which tells you which declension the noun belongs to. The third (ἡ) is the article, which tells you the gender — feminine in this case.

Second, the numbered senses. BDAG organizes its entries by distinct semantic domains, numbered in order. A word may have three or fifteen distinct senses, and which sense applies in a given passage is always a matter of exegetical judgment — one that the commentator must make and defend.

Third, the citations. Each sense is supported by examples from the NT and other literature. These are not decoration; they are the evidence. Looking up the cited passages and reading them in context is how you verify the lexicographer's claim.

Other essential tools

Thayer's Greek-English Lexicon — older (1889), based on the TR text tradition, but still useful and more accessible than BDAG for entry-level work. Available free online.

Liddell-Scott-Jones (LSJ) — the comprehensive classical Greek lexicon. Useful when NT Greek terms have roots in classical literature and you want the full semantic history. Very large; available free through the Perseus Digital Library.

Moulton and Milligan — The Vocabulary of the Greek New Testament, documenting Koine Greek as it appears in papyri (business documents, letters, contracts) from the same era as the NT. Invaluable for showing that NT vocabulary is ordinary Hellenistic usage, not a specialized "sacred" dialect.

MorphGNT — a digital database providing morphological analysis (parsing) for every word in the Greek NT. Used in the preparation of this commentary to audit and verify Greek forms and their correct identification.

Section V

The Transmission of the Greek New Testament

The New Testament documents were written in the first century. The oldest surviving manuscript fragments date to the early second century — within living memory of the apostolic generation. What lies between those original documents and the manuscripts we possess is the history of textual transmission: thousands of scribes, across fifteen centuries, hand-copying these texts in monasteries, scriptoria, and private homes across the ancient world.

The manuscript tradition

Over 5,800 Greek manuscripts of the New Testament have been identified — more than for any other text of antiquity by an enormous margin. They are catalogued by type.

Papyri (ℙ)
Written on papyrus; typically the oldest witnesses. ℙ⁵² (Rylands Papyrus), containing John 18:31–33, 37–38, dates to approximately AD 125 — within a generation of the original composition. Over 140 papyri have been identified.
Uncials (Majuscules)
Written in capital letters on vellum (treated animal skin). Include the great fourth-century codices: Codex Sinaiticus (ℵ) and Codex Vaticanus (B) — the two most important witnesses to the NT text. Both complete or near-complete Bibles.
Minuscules
Written in a flowing cursive script on vellum; ninth century and later. Numerically the largest category — over 2,800 identified. Most represent the Byzantine text tradition.
Lectionaries
Service books containing NT passages arranged for liturgical reading. Over 2,400 identified. Valuable for establishing the text in specific geographic and liturgical traditions.
Early Versions (Translations)
Ancient translations into Latin (Vetus Latina, Vulgate), Syriac (Peshitta, Harklean), Coptic, Armenian, and others. Witness to the Greek text available in their regions and periods, often earlier than surviving Greek manuscripts.
Church Fathers (Patristic Citations)
NT quotations in the writings of early Christian authors (Clement, Origen, Tertullian, Augustine, Chrysostom, etc.). When a Father quotes a passage before the date of our earliest manuscript, his citation is evidence for the text in his time and place.

Manuscript families and text types

Manuscripts are not scattered randomly. They cluster into families that share common readings, reflecting their descent from common ancestors. Text critics identify four primary text types for the NT.

How textual criticism works

When manuscripts disagree on a reading, textual critics evaluate the competing options using two categories of evidence: external (age, geographic distribution, and text type of the supporting manuscripts) and internal (which reading better explains the origin of the other — that is, which reading a scribe was more likely to have introduced accidentally or deliberately).

The governing principle of internal evidence is sometimes summarized as: prefer the reading that best explains the others. Scribes were far more likely to smooth out difficulties than to introduce them, so the harder reading — all else being equal — is generally more likely to be original. They also harmonized parallel passages and expanded short readings. The critical apparatus at the bottom of a scholarly Greek NT page documents the variant readings and their manuscript support for every significant textual decision in the text above.

Excursus

The stability of the New Testament text

It is worth pausing to observe what the manuscript tradition actually demonstrates. The scale of agreement across five thousand-plus manuscripts, separated by centuries and geography, is extraordinary. The text-critical scholar Bruce Metzger estimated that approximately 97–99% of the NT text is established beyond reasonable doubt. The remaining variants — the 1–3% that constitute real textual uncertainty — affect no major doctrine and concern mostly matters of phrasing, order, and the inclusion or exclusion of short passages. The New Testament we possess is, by any reasonable standard, an accurate transmission of the documents the apostles and their associates produced. Textual criticism is not an enemy of confidence in Scripture; it is the discipline that grounds that confidence in evidence rather than assumption.

Section VI

Key Greek Terms in This Commentary

The terms below recur throughout this commentary with doctrinal precision. Each entry gives the Greek form, transliteration, morphological category, and definition as used in the context of this commentary's theological vocabulary. This list is not exhaustive; it is a working index of the terms a reader will encounter most frequently.

Greek Transliteration Definition and Usage
ἀγαθός agathos Adjective. Intrinsic good — good in itself, by nature. Distinguished from καλός (beautiful, attractive good). In Romans 8:28, God works all things for ἀγαθόν — not merely pleasant good but absolute good of enduring value.
ἀγαπάω / ἀγάπη agapaō / agapē Verb / Noun. Love directed by will and principle rather than emotion or attraction. Impersonal love for all mankind as a category — the love that does not depend on the worthiness of the object but on the virtue of the subject. Contrasted with φιλέω (personal affection). The distinction carries full doctrinal weight in John 21 and throughout the Pauline corpus.
δικαιοσύνη dikaiosynē Noun, feminine. Righteousness — the absolute righteousness of God, the standard by which all things are measured; and the righteousness imputed to the believer at salvation (Rom. 1:17; 3:21–22; 4:3–5). The Greek root is δίκη — justice. Δικαιοσύνη and δικαίωσις (justification) are cognates, making explicit the link between righteousness and the judicial act of justification that English translations partially obscure.
ἐπίγνωσις epignōsis Noun, feminine. Full, applied, directed knowledge. The compound of ἐπί (upon, at, toward) + γνῶσις (knowledge). Where γνῶσις is academic, apprehended knowledge in the left lobe, ἐπίγνωσις is doctrine transferred to and resident in the right lobe — metabolized, applied, producing momentum. The goal of Operation Z. Eph. 1:17; Col. 1:9–10; 2 Pet. 1:2–3.
εὐαγγέλιον euangelion Noun, neuter. Good news — the gospel. Εὖ (well, good) + ἄγγελος (messenger, message). In the Roman world, the term was used for imperial proclamations — announcements of victory, accession, or peace. Paul's use of the word in Romans 1:1–17 sets the gospel against the backdrop of a competing imperial claim: there is a different king, a different victory, a different righteousness than Caesar's.
ζωή zōē Noun, feminine. Life — specifically the divine, spiritual, eternal life of God. Distinguished from βίος (biological, physical life) and ψυχή (soul-life). The gift imputed to the human spirit at regeneration. Αἰώνιος ζωή (aiōnios zōē) — eternal life — is not merely unending duration but life of a divine quality.
καρδία kardia Noun, feminine. Heart — used throughout the NT for the right lobe of the soul, the seat of volition, faith, and the storage of ἐπίγνωσις doctrine. Not the emotions (which in Greek anthropology are located in the κοιλία, σπλάγχνον, and νεφρός). The καρδία is the organ of spiritual life, character formation, and communion with God.
κοιλία koilia Noun, feminine. Literally the hollow of the body — the abdomen, stomach, womb. Used metaphorically for the seat of impulsive feeling and emotional response (the solar plexus). In Phil. 3:19 and Rom. 16:18, the cosmic believer makes his κοιλία his god — he lives from his emotions rather than from doctrine in the καρδία.
λόγος logos Noun, masculine. Word, reason, discourse, the divine reason underlying all reality. In John 1:1–14, the eternal, personal, divine Λόγος who became flesh. In NT epistolary use, often simply "the word" of God — the content of divine revelation as communicated through Scripture and teaching. Ῥήματα (rhēmata) and λόγος are related but distinct: ῥήματα often refers to specific sayings or utterances, λόγος to the larger message or account.
μὴ γένοιτο mē genoito Optative construction. Literally "may it not come to be" — Paul's emphatic rhetorical negation, used fourteen times in the NT, thirteen of them in Paul (ten in Romans alone). No English rendering fully captures its force. "God forbid!" (KJV), "By no means!" (ESV), "Certainly not!" (NKJV). The optative mood makes it a strong wish or strong rejection: the absolute repudiation of a false inference.
νοῦς nous Noun, masculine. Mind — the left lobe of the soul, the organ of cognitive apprehension. Receives doctrine first as γνῶσις. The νοῦς must be renewed (Rom. 12:2; Eph. 4:23) — a process that occurs through the consistent intake of doctrine under the enabling power of the Holy Spirit.
ὁμολογέω homologeō Verb. To say the same thing — to name, to acknowledge, to cite. Used in 1 John 1:9 for the confession of sin: naming or citing the sin to God the Father. The mechanics of rebound. Carries no emotional weight in the Greek — it is a judicial act of identification, not a performance of contrition. Ὁμολογία (homologia) = confession, acknowledgment.
πίστις pistis Noun, feminine. Faith — trust, reliance, conviction. Non-meritorious by nature: it is not the faith that saves but the object of faith (Christ). In Eph. 2:8, διὰ πίστεως — through faith — faith is the channel, not the ground, of salvation. Also used objectively for "the faith" — the body of Christian doctrine (Jude 3; Gal. 1:23).
πνεῦμα pneuma Noun, neuter. Spirit — used in the NT for God the Holy Spirit, for the human spirit created at regeneration, and for spirit beings. The human πνεῦμα is the organ of spiritual perception and the recipient of eternal life at salvation. The spiritual phenomena of God's Word are transmitted through the human spirit before reaching the νοῦς.
σάρξ sarx Noun, feminine. Flesh — used both literally (physical flesh, body) and theologically for the old sin nature (the sin capacity inherited from Adam, residing in the cell structure of the physical body). When Paul writes "according to the flesh" (κατὰ σάρκα), he means operating from the sin nature rather than from the Spirit — production outside the Divine Dynasphere.
σπλάγχνον splanchnon Noun, neuter (usually plural). Entrails, viscera — the seat of strong emotion and passionate impulse in Greek anthropology (analogous to the heart in modern English idiom, but more visceral). In 2 Cor. 6:12, emotional response to people that crowds out receptivity to doctrine.
σῴζω sōzō Verb. To save, to rescue, to preserve from danger. In Eph. 2:8, the perfect passive participle σεσῳσμένοι — "you have been saved with the result that you remain saved" — is the foundation of eternal security. The perfect tense makes explicit what a simple past tense would leave open: the salvation effected at the moment of faith produces a permanently established state.
χάρις charis Noun, feminine. Grace — the unmerited favor of God operating through His justice, totally apart from human merit or effort. Not an emotion or an attitude in God but a policy: the justice of God imputing blessing to the indwelling righteousness of God in the believer, completely bypassing human worthiness. The mechanism of every divine blessing from salvation through supergrace.
ψυχή psychē Noun, feminine. Soul — the immaterial component of the human being that constitutes personality, consciousness, and volitional capacity. The seat of the νοῦς and καρδία. In Greek anthropology, distinguished from both the body (σῶμα) and the spirit (πνεῦμα). The unbeliever is ψυχικός (soulish) — lacking a human spirit, unable to receive spiritual phenomena (1 Cor. 2:14).
Section VII

For Further Study

The following resources are recommended for the reader who wishes to pursue Greek study beyond what this guide provides. They are arranged from most accessible to most technical.

Beginning Greek

William D. Mounce, Basics of Biblical Greek Grammar (Zondervan, 4th ed., 2019). The most widely used introductory grammar in evangelical seminaries. Clear, methodical, and available with an accompanying workbook and online resources. An honest year of work with Mounce produces genuine reading ability.

J. Gresham Machen, New Testament Greek for Beginners (1923; various reprints). Older and more rigorous than Mounce, but still in use in many classical programs. Available free in several digital formats. Machen expects more of the student and rewards the effort.

Intermediate and Reference

Daniel B. Wallace, Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics (Zondervan, 1996). The standard intermediate grammar — exhaustive treatment of Greek syntax with thorough exegetical examples. The reference to which working scholars turn when a grammatical question requires a definitive answer.

A.T. Robertson, A Grammar of the Greek New Testament in the Light of Historical Research (1914). A monument of scholarship — over 1,400 pages. Robertson's knowledge of the full range of Greek literature was extraordinary. Not a teaching grammar but an encyclopedic reference.

Lexical Tools

BDAG — A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament and Other Early Christian Literature, Bauer-Danker-Arndt-Gingrich, 3rd edition (University of Chicago Press, 2000). The professional standard. Available in Logos, Accordance, and other digital platforms.

Thayer's Greek-English Lexicon. Available free at BlueLetterBible.org and other online platforms. Older and based on the TR, but readable and useful for entry-level reference.

Strong's Exhaustive Concordance. Not a lexicon in the technical sense, but an indispensable index: every word in the KJV mapped to its Greek (or Hebrew) original with a reference number. The Strong's numbers are used across dozens of online and print tools as a cross-referencing system. Useful for finding where a Greek word appears; not adequate for determining what it means.

Textual Criticism

Bruce M. Metzger, The Text of the New Testament: Its Transmission, Corruption, and Restoration (4th ed., with Bart Ehrman, Oxford, 2005). The standard introduction to NT textual criticism — authoritative, balanced, and accessible to the educated non-specialist.

Bruce M. Metzger, A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament (2nd ed., UBS, 1994). The official commentary on the textual decisions made in the UBS/NA critical text. Explains the reasoning behind every significant text-critical decision, passage by passage.

Digital Tools

Logos Bible Software and Accordance are the two professional-grade platforms for biblical languages study. Both provide morphologically tagged Greek texts (meaning every word is parsed for you), linked to BDAG and other lexicons, with parallel Bible views and extensive search capabilities. The investment is substantial but repays serious long-term study.

Blue Letter Bible (blueletterbible.org) — free, web-based, with Strong's numbers, Thayer's, and basic parsing for every NT word. An excellent starting point for readers without access to professional platforms.

Perseus Digital Library (perseus.tufts.edu) — free access to LSJ, classical Greek texts, and grammatical tools. Essential for anyone tracing a word's full semantic history outside the NT.

The purpose of all these tools, in the end, is the same: to close the distance between the reader and the text — to let the words speak as precisely and fully as the Spirit who inspired them intended. The effort is worth it. The Word of God is worth it.
